⚡ Maxwell's Equations ⚡

📅 10 April 2026 🏷️ Electromagnetism
"And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there were Maxwell's equations."
\[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}\]
\[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\]
\[\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\]
\[\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\]

Differential form • International System

1. Historical Introduction

In 1865, James Clerk Maxwell published "A Dynamical Theory of the Electromagnetic Field", unifying electricity, magnetism, and optics into a single theory. His greatest contribution was the displacement current term \( \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t} \),who predicted the existence of electromagnetic waves.

🔬 Historic fact: Maxwell calculated the speed of these waves using \( c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\epsilon_0} \) and obtained ≈ 3×10⁸ m/s, coinciding with measurements of the speed of light. He concluded: "Light is an electromagnetic disturbance".

2. The Four Equations (Differential Form)

Gauss Law (Electric)

\[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}\]

Electric charges are sources of the electric field. Field lines originate at positive charges and terminate at negative charges.

Gauss Law (Magnetic)

\[\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\]

Magnetic monopoles do not exist. Magnetic field lines are always closed loops.

Faraday-Lenz Law

\[\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\]

A time-varying magnetic field induces a rotational electric field.

Ampère-Maxwell Law

\[\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\]

Electric currents and changing electric fields generate a magnetic field.

3. Integral Form

Applying Gauss's \(\left(\int_V (\nabla\cdot \vec{v})\,dV = \oint_S \vec{v} \cdot d\vec{a}\right)\) and Stokes' theorems \(\left(\int_S (\nabla\times \vec{v})\cdot d\vec{a} = \oint_C \vec{v} \cdot d\vec{l}\right)\), we obtain the integral form:

Differential Form Integral Form
\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}\) \(\oint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_{\text{enc}}}{\epsilon_0}\)
\(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\) \(\oint_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = 0\)
\(\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}\) \(\oint_C \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\frac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{A}\)
\(\nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}\) \(\oint_C \mathbf{B} \cdot d\mathbf{l} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}} + \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{d}{dt}\int_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A}\)
